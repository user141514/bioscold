## 6. Conclusion

We introduced a leakage-controlled benchmark and a candidate-level scoring framework for closed-vocabulary scaffold-conditioned fragment replacement. The final 77-feature scorer (D4S31) achieves Top-10 = 0.9243 on 13,347 secondary blind queries, a Δ = +0.0686 improvement over the strongest base-ranker baseline. The central methodological finding is that the most impactful feature-engineering decision is a deletion: removing five per-query prior-rank features eliminates a non-transferable shortcut, improving blind Top-10 by +0.0392 and reducing baseline hit loss 5.9×. We trace this to a generalizable design principle---sparse prior-score rank transforms can become generalization hazards under fragment distribution shift---and demonstrate that the complementary finding, that candidate-level scoring succeeds where query-level routing consistently fails, reflects a structural property of the task: the predictive signal resides in individual query-candidate interactions, not in query-level summaries.

The dual-mode workflow translates these ranking improvements into a practical review framework. By stratifying Exploration Mode proposals into provenance groups with distinct structural alert profiles, it provides medicinal chemists with an actionable risk structure for triaging computational suggestions.

Several limitations define the scope of these contributions. The benchmark uses structure-derived labels from a single data source and does not establish activity preservation. The prior_ranks removal was identified post-audit and is not a fully prospective feature-selection result. The A4C proxy provides computational screening signals that have not been validated against experimental off-target profiling. Addressing these limitations---through independent replication with pre-registered protocols, activity-based evaluation endpoints, and expert review calibration---constitutes the natural next phase of this work.
